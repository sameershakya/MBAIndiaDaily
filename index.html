Global Markets

South Korea's stock market triggered its circuit breaker twice in one day.
Samsung and SK Hynix each crashed 12%.

On June 23, the South Korean KOSPI index plunged nearly 10%, triggering market-wide circuit breakers twice — a rare occurrence that temporarily halted all trading. Samsung Electronics fell 12.3% and SK Hynix dropped 12.5%. Foreign investors sold a net 5.79 trillion Korean won (~$3.8 billion) of shares in a single session. Three things hit simultaneously: South Korea was excluded from the MSCI Developed Markets review, US tech stocks fell overnight, and leveraged AI-related positions were forcibly unwound.

Why it matters
Samsung and SK Hynix together make nearly half of KOSPI’s total market value. When two companies cause a market-wide circuit breaker, the rest of the world’s markets notice. India felt it immediately — Sensex fell 893 points the same day.
Manager takeaway
Markets that rise 90%+ in five months on a single theme (AI chips) accumulate enormous positional risk. When the thesis is questioned, the fall is proportional to the rise. Concentration in one theme is not conviction — it is exposure.
Source: Sahi.com, TradingKey, Univest.in, Freepressjournal
AI Chips

Tonight, one American chip company reports earnings.
And the entire global AI trade is riding on that single number.

Micron Technology — the only American company manufacturing High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) chips at scale — reports its Q3 FY2026 earnings tonight, after US markets close. Analysts expect revenue of ~$33.5 billion and a gross margin of 81%, up from 75% last quarter. HBM revenue already crossed $1 billion for the first time in Q2. Tonight, investors need to see acceleration. Micron’s entire 2026 HBM output is already pre-sold under long-term agreements.

Why it matters
HBM chips sit physically inside every AI server Nvidia ships. Without HBM, AI cannot scale. Only three companies in the world make it. If Micron’s numbers disappoint tonight, investors will question whether the AI infrastructure boom has run ahead of reality. If they beat, Asia markets should recover sharply tomorrow.
Manager takeaway
Watch the HBM-specific guidance number tonight, not just the headline revenue. If demand for AI memory is softening, Micron will signal it in its forward guidance. A revenue beat with weak guidance is more bearish than a miss with confident guidance.
Source: TheStreet, TechTimes, IndMoney, Micron IR, FXStreet
Markets

Sensex fell 893 points yesterday.
IT and metals dragged. Only pharma went up.
Here is what the market was actually saying.

The BSE Sensex closed at 76,200 on June 23 — down 893 points (1.16%). The Nifty50 fell 279 points. IT giants Infosys, Wipro and TCS led the losers. Metal stocks (Hindalco, JSW Steel) followed. Why it matters
When every sector falls except pharma and healthcare, the market is telling you something: investors are moving out of cyclical (tech, metal) and into defensive (medicine, utilities). This is called defensive rotation and it happens when investors expect more volatility ahead.
Manager takeaway
If Micron’s earnings tonight are strong, expect Indian IT to recover sharply on Thursday. If Micron disappoints, the IT selloff accelerates. Today’s India market direction is entirely in the hands of one American chip company reporting after US hours tonight.

Mobility

Rapido just raised $240 million and is now valued at $3 billion.
India’s largest bike taxi company has a bigger story than its name suggests.

Rapido, India’s largest bike taxi and auto-rickshaw aggregation platform, raised $240 million in fresh funding, valuing it at $3 billion. Rapido operates across 100+ cities, completes millions of rides daily on two-wheelers and three-wheelers, and has built a business model designed around India’s urban reality: most people cannot afford ₹200+ for a cab, but a ₹50 bike ride works perfectly for short city commutes.

Why it matters
Rapido solved a problem Ola and Uber refused to: affordable last-mile mobility for India’s middle and lower-middle class. Ola and Uber optimised for the car-riding urban professional. Rapido optimised for the person who commutes 3 km to a metro station every day. Two completely different markets, same country.
Manager takeaway
The $3B valuation proves that “boring” markets — auto-rickshaws and bike taxis — produce large companies when executed at scale with good unit economics. The market Rapido serves is 10x larger than the premium cab market. Volume at thin margins beats niche at high margins at India scale.
Source: IndianStartupNews.com, Entrackr

India Economy

Energy Diplomacy

India officially welcomed the US-Iran peace MoU — and called the Hormuz opening a strategic win

  • What happened? India’s Ministry of External Affairs issued an official statement welcoming the US-Iran peace MoU, specifically calling the Strait of Hormuz reopening “a positive development for global energy security and trade.” The statement was more than diplomatic courtesy — it was an economic calculation.
  • Why managers care: India’s official endorsement of the peace MoU signals that New Delhi is calibrating its energy diplomacy carefully. A stable Hormuz means stable oil imports, which directly affects India’s inflation trajectory, fuel prices, and the current account deficit. Energy security is the silent variable behind almost every major Indian economic policy decision.
  • Consumer feel: The government’s official optimism about energy security is a signal that it expects fuel price relief to sustain — which would translate to lower petrol, diesel and LPG prices for Indian households in the coming weeks.

Ecosystem Capital

Startup India Fund of Funds 2.0 is now official — ₹10,000 crore of patient capital for startups

  • What happened? The Government of India officially notified the Startup India FoF 2.0 in the Gazette with a corpus of ₹10,000 crore. Unlike direct government grants, this is a Fund of Funds model — the government invests in venture capital funds, which then invest in startups. It is designed to mobilise ₹4 of private capital for every ₹1 of public money.
  • Why managers care: A Fund of Funds structure is more market-efficient than direct government startup grants. The government does not pick winners — professional VCs do. The government just de-risks the VC’s own LP commitments, which unlocks more private capital. It is one of the smarter structures in startup policy globally.
  • Consumer feel: This fund will directly help build the startups that create the products you will use 5-7 years from now — in healthcare, agriculture, clean energy and deep tech sectors that private capital alone does not fund at early stages.
Source: PIB India, DPIIT Gazette notification
Current Account

India's Q1 FY27 current account is a mixed picture — oil relief on one side, weak rupee on the other

  • What happened? India enters Q1 FY27 (April–June 2026) with three competing forces: cheap crude oil reducing the import bill, a weak rupee at ₹95.38/USD making all dollar-denominated imports more expensive, and FII outflows from the Korean/tech selloff adding more rupee pressure. The net effect is not clean relief — it is a partial offset.
  • Why managers care: The current account deficit is India’s structural vulnerability. When oil is cheap but the rupee is weak, the savings on the oil bill are partially eaten by the forex cost of paying for it. Businesses that import in dollars need to model both variables simultaneously, not just track oil prices.
  • Consumer feel: The complex interplay of oil prices and rupee value means consumer price relief may take longer to arrive than the oil crash headline suggests — the currency effect is quietly working in the opposite direction.
Source: HDFCSky, Angel One, RBI DBIE

🇹🇷 MSCI excluded South Korea from Developed Markets review — India may be the beneficiary

If India’s EM weight increases, foreign institutional investors must buy Indian stocks regardless of their own view on India’s economy.

AI Supply Chain

Block Deal

Vedanta promoters sold ₹2,100 crore of shares in one day — what a block deal actually is and why it matters

  • What happened? Vedanta’s promoters (Anil Agarwal’s family and holding companies) sold a stake worth over ₹2,100 crore through a block deal on the stock exchange on June 23. Vedanta’s stock fell around 6% on the news. Block deals are pre-negotiated bulk transactions between large buyers and sellers that happen before market hours.
  • Finance manager view: Block deals by promoters are watched carefully because they signal promoter-level thinking about the company’s valuation and their own liquidity needs. A large promoter stake sale is not always bearish — it sometimes happens for estate planning or debt repayment — but it naturally increases share supply in the near term, which pressures the price.
  • Consumer impact: If you own Vedanta shares or an index fund that includes them, yesterday’s 6% drop affected your portfolio. Understanding block deals helps you distinguish between fundamental deterioration and technical supply-driven price moves.

Zepto is slowing down its dark store expansion ahead of its IPO — and the reason is a cash runway calculation

  • What happened? According to Entrackr, Zepto has been slowing its dark store (micro-warehouse) expansion as it conserves cash ahead of its ₹8,010 crore IPO. Zepto is trading short-term operational momentum for IPO readiness.
  • Watch: Whether Zepto resumes aggressive dark store expansion immediately post-IPO using the raised capital, or whether the slowdown reflects a strategic rethink of its geographic expansion model towards fewer, higher-density stores.

HR & Leadership

Talent Gap

India has zero HBM chip manufacturers. And the world's AI economy is entirely dependent on this one type of chip.

  • What happened? As the KOSPI crash reminded the world, only three companies supply all HBM chips globally. India has none. No Indian company designs, manufacturers or tests High Bandwidth Memory at any commercial scale. As India builds AI infrastructure, it will remain entirely import-dependent for this critical component unless it starts building the talent and industrial base now.
  • Future manager lesson: The most valuable skills in the AI economy are not “prompt engineering” or “AI product management” — those are relatively replicable. The genuinely scarce skills are semiconductor process engineering, lithography, chip design and materials science. India has strong software talent and very thin hardware talent. That gap is a strategic vulnerability and a career opportunity simultaneously.
  • Career implication: Semiconductor engineering roles in India currently command among the highest compensation premiums of any technical discipline, with very few qualified candidates competing for each position. If you have an engineering background, pivoting toward chip design or semiconductor manufacturing is one of the best-rewarded moves you can make in 2026.

Technology & AI

AI Infrastructure

HBM: the chip that runs the entire AI economy. Most people have never heard of it.

  • What happened? High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) is a type of DRAM chip that sits physically on top of AI processor packages from Nvidia, AMD and Google. Without HBM, no modern AI training or inference at scale is possible. Micron’s entire 2026 HBM supply is already pre-sold. Samsung and SK Hynix’s entire output is committed to Nvidia under long-term agreements. If demand exceeds what these three companies can produce, AI infrastructure spending physically cannot grow faster than HBM production.
  • Future jobs: HBM chip production requires a new type of specialist: someone who understands both advanced memory process design (a materials science and physics domain) and AI system architecture requirements. This is a discipline that does not exist in most engineering curricula yet. The people who develop it will name their price for the next decade.
  • Business opportunity: India’s government AI centres, the Meta-Reliance Jamnagar hub and every future Indian AI project will be paying premium prices for HBM chips from Korean and American companies indefinitely unless India starts building semiconductor capacity. That gap is both a vulnerability and the single biggest manufacturing opportunity in the country.
Source: TechInsider.org, IndMoney, Micron IR, TheStreet
Next-Gen AI

Micron’s HBM4 is being built for Nvidia’s “Rubin” — the next generation of AI chips that makes today’s Blackwell look old

  • What happened? Micron is developing HBM4 — the next generation of high-bandwidth memory — specifically for Nvidia’s upcoming “Rubin” AI architecture, announced at GTC 2026 and expected for H2 2026. HBM4 provides even higher bandwidth and capacity than HBM3E (the current standard), and each Rubin GPU will use more HBM4 stacks than the current Blackwell generation uses of HBM3E.
  • Future jobs: Each new GPU generation requiring more HBM creates a compounding demand problem. More chips, more stacks, more bandwidth required. The engineers who can design and test next-generation memory stacking processes are entering one of the most supply-constrained job markets in the world.
  • Business opportunity: The jump from HBM3E to HBM4 is not just a spec improvement — it is a structural demand step-up. Every AI data centre built with Rubin GPUs will need 30-50% more HBM content per server. This is why Micron’s 2026 HBM output is already committed before the year ends.
Source: TechInsider.org, TheStreet, Micron IR investor materials

  • Future jobs: Institutional fintech infrastructure requires engineers who combine deep knowledge of financial market microstructure (how forex actually trades, clears and settles) with distributed systems engineering (how you build reliable, low-latency systems at global scale). This intersection is where the highest-paid fintech engineering roles sit.
  • Business opportunity: As the rupee trades at historically weak levels and more Indian companies borrow or earn in foreign currencies, professional forex risk management infrastructure becomes more critical. OpenFX is building the plumbing that Indian exporters, importers and financial institutions will rely on to manage currency exposure more precisely.

Manager's Takeaways

Insight 01

🧠 When every sector falls and one sector rises, that rising sector is telling you something about the future, not just the present.

  • Business lesson: Defensive sectors outperform in volatile markets not because they are exciting but because their demand is inelastic. People do not stop buying medicine or paying electricity bills when markets crash. The sectors that hold steady during a selloff are the same sectors that compound quietly during growth years — they just never get the headlines.
  • Manager takeaway: In portfolio construction, career planning and business strategy, always ask: which parts of my plan are inelastic (remain valuable regardless of macro conditions) and which are cyclical (perform well only when conditions are favourable)? Building more inelastic elements into any plan makes it more durable.
  • Real world check: Pharma and utilities rising yesterday while tech crashed is not new. It has happened in every correction of the last 30 years. The pattern is reliable enough to plan around. Boring, essential businesses consistently outlast exciting, discretionary ones over long periods.
Insight 02

🧠 Three companies controlling 97% of a critical input is not a market — it is a geopolitical fact dressed as a market.

  • Business lesson: When three suppliers control virtually all supply of something the entire world needs, pricing power is absolute, alternatives are non-existent and dependency is structural. This is not competition — it is an oligopoly with geopolitical implications. Any country that depends on imports from three suppliers for a critical technology is one diplomatic incident away from an infrastructure crisis.
  • Manager takeaway: Map the critical inputs to your business or sector and count how many independent suppliers exist for each. If the number is 1-3 globally, that is a strategic risk that belongs in every business plan and every supply chain analysis. Diversification of critical inputs is not a cost — it is insurance.
  • Real world check: India imports all its oil from multiple suppliers, creating manageable dependency. India imports all its HBM chips from three suppliers, creating absolute dependency. The difference between “many suppliers” and “three suppliers” is the difference between a cost and an existential risk.
Insight 03

🧠 Capital fixes the balance sheet. Only a working business model fixes the business.

  • Business lesson: Injecting capital into a company with a broken business model creates a better-capitalised broken company, not a fixed one. The market prices this correctly when it sells the stock despite the capital infusion. Money buys time, but it does not buy customers, product-market fit or a sustainable unit economics model. Those have to be built.
  • Manager takeaway: When evaluating whether to join, invest in or lend to a company, always ask: is this company asking for capital to accelerate something that already works, or to fund losses while hoping something that does not yet work eventually will? The first is a reasonable bet. The second is a hope dressed as a strategy.
  • Real world check: This applies to your own career too. Getting a new certification, an MBA or a networking event does not fix a career that lacks direction. Skills and credentials are capital. But only a clear direction — the business model of your career — turns that capital into value.
Insight 04

🧠 A market nobody wanted is often the biggest market of all. Volume at thin margins almost always beats niche at high margins in India.

  • Business lesson: Rapido’s $3B valuation comes from serving a customer segment that Ola and Uber decided was too low-value: the person who travels 3 km on a bike instead of 10 km in a cab. The “boring” segment turned out to be 10x larger than the premium one. This pattern repeats across India: the mass market is almost always larger than the premium market, and both Ola and Uber underestimated it.
  • Manager takeaway: In India specifically, the mass market is structurally underserved relative to the premium market. Most startup capital, talent and attention flows toward the premium urban consumer. But the mass market — affordable food, affordable mobility, affordable housing — often offers larger absolute scale with lower competitive intensity from well-funded startups.
  • Real world check: Zepto is a ₹22,000+ crore revenue company. Flipkart is worth tens of billions. Both serve the same mass consumer. Neither built that scale by targeting the premium consumer exclusively. India’s scale is in the middle and the mass market, not in the top 5%.
Insight 05

🧠 What you specifically know deeply is more fundable than what everyone knows generally. Depth has a moat. Breadth does not.

  • Business lesson: A naval architect who starts an autonomous ships company has a moat that an MBA generalist starting “an AI startup for defence” cannot replicate. The domain knowledge creates a barrier to imitation that general management skills do not. In a world where AI can perform many generalist functions, deep domain expertise is becoming more valuable, not less.
  • Manager takeaway: The most valuable thing you can do in the next 3-5 years is develop a specific expertise that is genuinely hard to replicate. Not broader knowledge, but deeper knowledge in one area. The person who understands India’s wedding logistics better than anyone, or India’s babycare consumer better than anyone, or India’s maritime border challenges better than anyone — that person has optionality that a generalist does not.
  • Real world check: Zerodha built the most profitable Indian retail fintech by being the world’s best at one specific thing: low-cost retail stock trading. Not fintech broadly. Not payments. Not lending. Just that one specific product, executed with absolute discipline. Depth at one thing compounds. Breadth across many things averages out.
